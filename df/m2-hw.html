occasional muskox posted at 2021-07-04T15:46:23.000Z

https://stackoverflow.com/questions/25663808/how-to-convert-negative-hexadecimal-to-decimal (Links to an external site.)

As mentioned by Summer and Phaniteja, you need to take the two's complement to find its absolute value. The simplest way to visualize this, shown above, is to subtract the representation from the first number past the total range of used digits; this accounts for an extra 1 in binary. In the example shown:

0x100000000

-0x0FFFFFE58

the first step in the subtraction is 0-8 (the rightmost digits); since this cannot be done, we borrow from the first available digit: the leading 1. Then, we borrow in each successive position, until we end up with 0FFFFFFF(16) [16 being used where the original 0 was, as we would do use 10 in place of 0 in normal decimal subtraction).

0FFFFFFF(16)

-0FFFFFE58

Now, from right to left,

16 - 8 =8,

F - 5 = (15) - 5 = 10 =A,

F - E = (15 - 14) =1,

F - F = (15 - 15) = 0,

and so on, until we get:

0x0000001A8

Once we have our hexadecimal representation, we can convert it to decimal by summing up each place appropriately. Since hexadecimal is base 16, going right-to-left, we have 16^0, 16^1, 16^2, and so on.

Here, we have

8 * (16^0) = 8 * 1 = 8

+

(A = 10)

10 * (16^1) = 10 * 16 = 160

+

1 * (16^2) = 256

= 8 + 160 + 256 = 424

And since this was negative, we get -424.

fixed frog posted at 2021-07-05T20:27:28.000Z
The point of the homework is to practice conversion. Continue to keep practicing to improve on future quizzes and exams.
Just remember to specify more on your question so others can better identify your issues and better help you.

In a general sense, conversions are common in the STEM field to help identify the numbers being used. For our homework, we are working with bases 2, 10, and 16; respectively binary, decimal, and hexadecimal. These are different ways to count. Decimal is engrained into us when using numbers, like how we used to count to 10 on our fingers. We can do the same with binary and hexadecimal. Binary is counted by 0 and 1's with 2^n. I recommend learning powers of 2s or just writing out decimals' value over binary numbers as they can be hard to read visually. Hex is n^16(i-1), where n is the hex number and i is the integer placement. 

Get used to counting in binary and hex, as it's easier to convert them directly to each other, than thinking in decimal notation.
